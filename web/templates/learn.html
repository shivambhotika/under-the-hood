How to Read This

Under The Hood in plain English — no technical background required.

What is this?

Under The Hood scans thousands of real software projects on GitHub and looks at what tools they have installed. Not what developers say they use. Not what's trending on social media. What's literally in their code.

Think of it like a supermarket scanner for software. Instead of checking what people say they're buying, we look at what's actually in their cart.

The key concepts

What is a 'repo' or 'repository'?

A repository is a project on GitHub — a collection of code, usually for one piece of software. "8,102 repos use Vitest" means 8,102 separate software projects have Vitest installed.

We only track repos with 500+ GitHub stars. Stars are roughly like likes: they indicate other developers found the project useful. This filter removes hobby projects and abandoned experiments.

What is a 'dependency'?

Developers do not write everything from scratch. They use pre-built tools — dependencies — for common tasks. Dependency files (like package.json or requirements.txt) list these tools.

Scanning dependencies means reading those lists across thousands of projects to see which tools appear most often.

What is 'adoption' and how is it different from popularity?

Popularity means people talk about it. Adoption means people actually use it in production code.

A tool can trend online but have low real-world use, or be heavily used and rarely discussed. This product measures adoption in code, not hype in conversation.

What is the 'Emergence Score'?

A tool with 200 projects growing 40% is often more interesting than a tool with 15,000 projects growing 2%. Emergence score rewards growth relative to size.

High emergence means fast growth from a smaller base (often an early signal). Low emergence plus high adoption usually means stable infrastructure.

What does 'market phase' mean?

Early / Competing: many tools exist, none clearly winning.

Consolidating: a winner is pulling ahead but has not fully won.

Mature: one tool has effectively won the category.

Fragmenting: many tools, but none gaining momentum.

What does this NOT tell you?

It only covers public code (not private enterprise repos). Being listed in dependencies does not guarantee active use in all features. 500+ star filtering improves quality but can miss very new tools.

This is not a preference survey. It is an install-base measurement.

What is The Radar?

The Radar is the section of this product most relevant to early-stage investors. It surfaces tools that meet a specific combination of criteria: small enough that most people have not heard of them, growing fast enough to suggest genuine momentum, and not yet backed by known institutional capital.

The historical pattern: OSS tools often win their category before anyone has raised money to commercialize them. Spark was the most active big-data project on GitHub in 2012. Databricks (the company) was founded in 2013 and raised from a16z. The people who found the Spark team in 2012 on GitHub had a year's head start.

The Radar tries to surface those 2012 moments, in real time.

Who is this for?

Developers: choose between competing tools using real adoption data.

Investors and analysts: open-source adoption often leads enterprise buying patterns.

The curious: understand what the software ecosystem is building right now without reading thousands of repositories.
